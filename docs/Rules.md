# Project S.T.A.C.K.

## Core Rules Draft: Medieval Fantasy Module

---

# 1. Game Overview

Project S.T.A.C.K. is a modular, cooperative tabletop adventure game that combines map exploration, character building, card-based combat, equipment, and dungeon delving.

Players control allied characters exploring a face-down world map. They discover terrain, settlements, dungeons, and special locations. Characters improve by finding loot, purchasing cards, equipping skills, and adapting their equipment to the challenges ahead.

The initial campaign objective is to clear all required dungeons on the map. Future scenarios may add quests, missions, bosses, timed objectives, or other victory conditions.

Most card-specific instructions are printed directly on cards. This rulebook provides the general rules that apply when a card does not say otherwise.

## Rule Priority

When rules conflict, use the following order:

1. Scenario rules.
2. Card text.
3. Core rules in this document.

A specific rule overrides a more general rule.

---

# 2. Core Concepts

## Players and Characters

Each player controls one character represented by a Character Card and that character's associated cards, equipment, tokens, and inventory.

The initial medieval fantasy classes are:

* Warrior
* Rogue
* Mage
* Cleric
* Druid

Each Character Card defines that character's:

* Maximum HP.
* Class traits.
* Native skill categories.
* Starting abilities or special rules.
* Other class-specific features.

Exact starting HP, traits, and starting cards are defined by the relevant Character Card.

---

## Cards

The game uses several card types, including:

* Character Cards
* Equipment Cards
* Skill Cards
* Spell Cards
* Ability Cards
* Item Cards
* Transportation Cards
* Enemy Cards
* Dungeon Cards
* Loot Cards

A card's type, tags, cost, effects, and restrictions are printed on that card.

---

## Tokens and Counters

The game may use tokens or counters to track:

* Damage.
* HP.
* Heat.
* Bleed.
* Currency.
* Scenario effects.
* Other card-specific effects.

---

## Discarding and Shuffling Back

Discarding and shuffling back are different.

### Discard

A discarded card is removed from its current deck or area.

For example, when an enemy is defeated, that enemy is placed in the Dungeon Discard Pile and cannot be encountered again during that dungeon attempt.

### Shuffle Back

A card that is shuffled back into a deck may be drawn again later.

For example, if an enemy is drawn but its preferred row is full, shuffle that enemy back into the dungeon deck.

---

# 3. Winning and Losing

## Victory

Players win when they complete the scenario's required objectives.

For the default campaign, players win when they clear all required dungeon tiles.

A dungeon may have a unique objective, such as:

* Defeat a boss.
* Recover artifacts.
* Survive a countdown.
* Destroy an object.
* Clear all enemies.
* Complete a scenario-specific task.

---

## Defeat

The scenario determines how the players can lose.

Possible defeat conditions include:

* All player characters die.
* A turn limit expires before all required dungeons are cleared.
* Enemy forces spread beyond a permitted limit.
* A scenario-specific failure condition occurs.

---

# 4. Character Area

Each character has a personal play area containing their Character Card, equipped cards, Tactical Reserve, and counters.

## Equipment Slots

A character may equip cards in the following slots:

* Head
* Neck
* Body
* Hands
* Feet
* Left Held Slot
* Right Held Slot
* Left Ring
* Right Ring
* Transportation

The Held Slots are used for weapons, shields, magical focuses, tools, and similar equipment.

A two-handed item occupies both Held Slots if its card says so.

The Hands slot represents gloves, gauntlets, or similar worn hand equipment. It is separate from the Held Slots.

---

## Armor and Defense

Armor and shields provide Defense.

| Armor Type               | Defense Bonus |
| ------------------------ | ------------: |
| Clothing                 |            +0 |
| Leather Armor            |            +1 |
| Scale Mail or Brigandine |            +2 |
| Chainmail                |            +3 |
| Full Plate               |            +4 |

Common shield values are:

| Shield Type                  | Defense Bonus |
| ---------------------------- | ------------: |
| Buckler                      |            +1 |
| Kite Shield or Square Shield |            +2 |

A character can normally reach up to 6 Defense through Full Plate and a +2 shield.

Effects such as spells, abilities, or special equipment may raise Defense beyond 6.

A character's Defense can never exceed 10.

> The exact rule for how Defense reduces or prevents incoming damage is still pending.

---

# 5. HP, Damage, and Character Defeat

Each character's maximum HP is printed on their Character Card.

When a character takes damage, place Damage counters on that character.

When a character's Damage counters equal or exceed their maximum HP, that character becomes **Downed**.

## Downed Characters

A Downed character:

* Cannot take actions.
* Remains in the encounter.
* Keeps all equipped cards and inventory.
* Is not immediately dead.
* Receives a Downed marker.

---

## Bleed Out

At the end of each complete encounter round, place 1 Bleed token on every Downed character.

A complete encounter round consists of:

1. Drawing and resolving the dungeon card.
2. Resolving all enemy turns.
3. Resolving all participating player character turns.

When a Downed character gains their third Bleed token, that character dies.

This gives allied characters three full encounter rounds to rescue them.

---

## Reviving a Downed Character

A Downed character may only be revived by another character using a spell, item, ability, or card effect that permits revival.

Unless the card says otherwise, reviving a character:

* Removes all Bleed tokens.
* Returns the character to 1 HP.
* Removes the Downed marker.

---

## Difficulty and Death Options

Players choose a death and recovery mode before starting a game.

### Heroic Mode

This is the recommended default mode.

* Characters become Downed at 0 HP.
* Characters die after gaining 3 Bleed tokens.
* Revival may be possible through cards or advanced medical services.

### Hardcore Mode

* Characters become Downed at 0 HP.
* Characters die after gaining 3 Bleed tokens.
* Dead characters cannot return to the game.

### Ironman Mode

* Characters die immediately when Damage equals or exceeds maximum HP.
* No Bleed Out period.
* No revival.

### Story Mode

* Characters do not permanently die.
* A character who reaches 3 Bleed tokens becomes unconscious and recovers after the encounter.

---

# 6. Skills, Spells, and Abilities

Each character has 5 Skill Slots.

A Skill Card represents training that the character is currently maintaining. Spell and Ability Cards represent the techniques, knowledge, or powers that training allows the character to use.

A Spell or Ability Card may only be equipped to a Skill Card if:

* Its tag matches that Skill Card.
* Its rank is not higher than the Skill Card's rank.
* The Skill Card has remaining capacity.

For example:

* A card tagged `Restoration Magic` may be equipped to a Restoration Magic Skill Card.
* A card tagged `Thief` may be equipped to a Thief Skill Card.
* A card with multiple tags may be equipped to any matching Skill Card.

---

## Skill Rank and Capacity

A Skill Card's rank determines:

* The highest rank of Spell or Ability Card it can equip.
* The total number of Spell or Ability Cards it can hold.

| Rank         | Maximum Equipped Cards |
| ------------ | ---------------------: |
| Novice       |                      1 |
| Apprentice   |                      2 |
| Adept        |                      3 |
| Master       |                      4 |
| Grand Master |                      5 |

For example, a Master Skill Card may equip up to 4 matching cards of Master rank or lower.

Those cards may be any mixture of lower ranks, provided the total does not exceed 4.

---

## Rank Names

Magic currently uses the following ranks:

* Novice
* Apprentice
* Adept
* Master
* Grand Master

Warrior skills currently use:

* Recruit
* Veteran
* Hero
* Champion
* Legend

Rogue, Cleric, and Druid rank naming is still being finalized.

Until specific rank names are established, those classes may use the standard Novice through Grand Master scale.

---

## Removing Skill Cards

When a Skill Card is removed, first return all attached Spell and Ability Cards to that character's Inventory.

The removed Skill Card is then resolved according to whether it is a Native Skill or an Off-Class Skill.

### Native Skills

A Native Skill belongs to the character's class.

For example, Magic skills are Native Skills for a Mage.

When a Native Skill is removed:

* The Skill Card returns to Inventory.
* Attached Spell and Ability Cards return to Inventory.
* The character does not lose the training card.

### Off-Class Skills

An Off-Class Skill does not belong to the character's class.

For example, a Mage using a Warrior Skill is using an Off-Class Skill.

When an Off-Class Skill is removed:

* Attached Spell and Ability Cards return to Inventory.
* The Off-Class Skill Card is discarded.

This represents the character no longer maintaining that foreign training.

---

## When Skills May Be Changed

Skill Cards may not be equipped or removed inside a dungeon.

Skill changes are normally performed at a settlement or another card-defined safe zone.

A settlement specialty determines which Off-Class Skills may be trained or equipped there.

---

# 7. Tactical Reserve and Inventory

## Tactical Reserve

Each character has 5 Tactical Reserve slots.

The Tactical Reserve holds consumable or tactical cards such as:

* Potions.
* Scrolls.
* Bombs.
* Bandages.
* Other single-use items.

Characters choose Tactical Reserve cards before entering a dungeon.

Once inside a dungeon, a character may use only the Tactical Reserve cards they brought with them.

Using a Tactical Reserve card normally costs 1 action unless the card says otherwise.

> The final name of Tactical Reserve may change later, but the rule remains the same.

---

## Inventory

Inventory represents the cards a character carries or has stored for later use.

Each character has a base Inventory capacity of 20 cards.

The following do not count against Inventory capacity:

* Equipped Equipment Cards.
* Equipped Skill Cards.
* Spell and Ability Cards attached to equipped Skill Cards.
* Tactical Reserve Cards.
* Currency tokens.
* Damage, Heat, or Bleed tokens.

Some cards increase Inventory capacity.

Examples:

* Backpack: +5 Inventory capacity.
* Caravan: +10 Inventory capacity.

A character may only access Inventory at a settlement or another card-defined safe location.

Inventory cannot normally be accessed inside a dungeon.

---

## Full Inventory

When a character's Inventory is full, that character cannot gain another Inventory card unless they first create space.

Possible ways to create space include:

* Selling cards.
* Discarding cards.
* Storing cards.
* Crafting with cards.
* Equipping an eligible card.
* Using a card that increases Inventory capacity.

> The exact rule for unclaimed dungeon loot when all participating characters have full Inventory is still pending.

---

## Equipment Changes

Equipment cannot normally be equipped, removed, or swapped inside a dungeon.

Equipment changes may be made only at settlements or other card-defined safe zones.

---

# 8. Currency and the Loot Deck

## Currency

Currency is represented by tokens, coins, or another setting-appropriate resource.

Currency is used for:

* Buying cards.
* Paying for medical care.
* Training.
* Crafting.
* Other settlement services.

Currency may be found in dungeons, earned through quests, gained through gathering or crafting, or sold from unwanted cards.

---

## The Shared Loot Deck

The Loot Deck is one shared deck containing all obtainable cards.

It may include:

* Weapons.
* Armor.
* Shields.
* Accessories.
* Transportation.
* Consumable items.
* Crafting materials.
* Skill Cards.
* Spell Cards.
* Ability Cards.
* Currency cards.
* Other treasure.

Every Loot Card has a printed currency cost.

The Loot Deck is used both by settlements and by dungeons.

When the Loot Deck becomes empty, shuffle its Loot Discard Pile to form a new Loot Deck.

---

# 9. World Map

The world map is a grid of square map spaces, approximately 20 by 20 spaces unless a scenario says otherwise.

Each map space contains one tile.

Tiles may represent:

* Open terrain.
* Difficult terrain.
* Water.
* Mountains.
* Villages.
* Towns.
* Cities.
* Dungeons.
* Special locations.

The starting region begins revealed.

All other map tiles begin face down.

---

# 10. Exploration

Characters do not automatically reveal tiles by moving onto them.

Instead, characters scout ahead before traveling into unknown territory.

## Adjacent Tiles

An adjacent tile is any tile directly next to the character's current map space, including diagonal spaces.

---

## Travel

When taking a Travel turn:

1. Reveal 1 adjacent face-down tile.
2. Spend Movement Points to move through revealed adjacent tiles.

A character may not enter a face-down tile unless a card specifically allows it.

---

## Scout

Instead of traveling, a character may Scout.

When taking a Scout turn:

* Do not move.
* Reveal 2 adjacent face-down tiles.

Scout effects represent searching the area, observing routes, or gathering information before committing to travel.

---

## Exploration Effects

Abilities and cards may modify exploration.

Examples:

* Scout: Reveal 2 additional tiles.
* Eagle Eye: Reveal 3 additional tiles.
* Ranger Trait: Whenever you reveal a tile, reveal 1 additional tile.

---

# 11. Terrain

Terrain changes the cost of travel.

Most difficult terrain costs 1 additional Movement Point.

| Terrain         | Normal Movement Cost | Terrain Category |
| --------------- | -------------------: | ---------------- |
| Open Field      |                    1 | Normal           |
| Road            |                    1 | Normal           |
| Forest          |                    2 | Dense            |
| Jungle          |                    2 | Dense            |
| Swamp           |                    2 | Wet              |
| Marsh           |                    2 | Wet              |
| Desert          |                    2 | Extreme          |
| Snow            |                    2 | Extreme          |
| Ice             |                    2 | Extreme          |
| Volcanic Region |                    2 | Extreme          |
| Water           |           Impassable | Water            |
| Mountain        |           Impassable | Mountain         |

Mountains cannot normally be entered.

Water cannot normally be entered without a suitable Transportation Card.

---

# 12. Transportation

Each character has 1 Transportation slot.

Transportation Cards change Movement Points, terrain access, Inventory capacity, stealth, or other travel rules.

No transportation option is intended to be universally best. Different terrain and objectives favor different travel methods.

---

## Traveling on Foot

Movement: 1

Benefits:

* Requires no equipment.
* Cannot be dismounted.
* May use Stealth.
* Ignores difficult terrain movement penalties.

When traveling on foot, Dense, Wet, and Extreme terrain each cost only 1 Movement Point.

A character on foot cannot enter Water or Mountain tiles.

---

## Draft Horse

Movement: 2

Benefits:

* +5 Inventory capacity.

Terrain Costs:

* Normal terrain: 1.
* Dense terrain: 2.
* Wet terrain: 2.
* Extreme terrain: 2.

A Draft Horse cannot enter Water or Mountain tiles.

---

## War Horse

Movement: 2

Benefits:

* May provide combat-oriented bonuses printed on the card.

Terrain Costs:

* Normal terrain: 1.
* Dense terrain: 2.
* Wet terrain: 2.
* Extreme terrain: 2.

A War Horse cannot enter Water or Mountain tiles.

---

## Giant Spider

Movement: 2

Benefits:

* Dense terrain costs 1.
* Wet terrain costs 1.
* May climb cliffs or obstacles when a card permits it.

Terrain Costs:

* Normal terrain: 1.
* Dense terrain: 1.
* Wet terrain: 1.
* Extreme terrain: 2.

A Giant Spider cannot enter Water or Mountain tiles unless a card specifically allows it.

---

## Camel

Movement: 2

Benefits:

* +3 Inventory capacity.
* Extreme terrain costs 1.

Terrain Costs:

* Normal terrain: 1.
* Dense terrain: 2.
* Wet terrain: 2.
* Extreme terrain: 1.

A Camel cannot enter Water or Mountain tiles.

---

## Dire Wolf

Movement: 2

Benefits:

* May grant Stealth benefits.
* Dense terrain costs 1.
* Snow and Ice cost 1.

Terrain Costs:

* Normal terrain: 1.
* Dense terrain: 1.
* Wet terrain: 2.
* Desert: 2.
* Snow and Ice: 1.

A Dire Wolf cannot enter Water or Mountain tiles.

---

## Flying Mount

Movement: 3

Benefits:

* Dense terrain costs 1.
* Wet terrain costs 1.
* Extreme terrain costs 1.
* May fly over Water.
* May fly over Mountains.

Restrictions:

* A Flying Mount may not end movement on a Mountain tile.
* A Flying Mount provides no Inventory bonus.
* A Flying Mount cannot benefit from Stealth.
* Flying Mounts are intended to be rare and expensive.

---

## Canoe

Movement: 1

Benefits:

* May travel on Rivers and Lakes.
* May carry up to 2 players.

A Canoe occupies a character's Transportation slot and may be carried while its owner is traveling on foot.

> Rules for passenger movement, embarking, disembarking, ships, and larger watercraft are still pending.

---

# 13. Settlements

Settlements provide safe zones where characters may access Inventory, manage equipment, buy cards, sell cards, receive medical care, and use specialty training.

Every settlement has:

* A size.
* A specialty.
* A market.

Settlement size determines the market size and general service level.

Settlement specialty determines discounts and skill-training access.

---

## Settlement Tiles

There are 15 Settlement Tiles.

| Specialty | Village         | Town         | City         |
| --------- | --------------- | ------------ | ------------ |
| Mage      | Mage Village    | Mage Town    | Mage City    |
| Warrior   | Warrior Village | Warrior Town | Warrior City |
| Rogue     | Rogue Village   | Rogue Town   | Rogue City   |
| Cleric    | Cleric Village  | Cleric Town  | Cleric City  |
| Druid     | Druid Village   | Druid Town   | Druid City   |

Each settlement tile has exactly 1 specialty.

There are no separate Trainer Cards or Guild Cards.

The tile itself determines the settlement's specialty.

---

## Village

Villages are small settlements.

Village Market:

* Reveal and maintain 4 Loot Cards.

Village Services:

* Inventory access.
* Equipment changes.
* Buying and selling.
* Storage.
* Basic medical care.
* Specialty training and discounts.

---

## Town

Towns are medium settlements.

Town Market:

* Reveal and maintain 8 Loot Cards.

Town Services:

* Inventory access.
* Equipment changes.
* Buying and selling.
* Storage.
* Improved medical care.
* Specialty training and discounts.

---

## City

Cities are large settlements.

City Market:

* Reveal and maintain 12 Loot Cards.

City Services:

* Inventory access.
* Equipment changes.
* Buying and selling.
* Storage.
* Advanced medical care.
* Specialty training and discounts.
* Advanced scenario services, such as crafting stations, blacksmiths, or special quest services.

Cities are intended to be rare.

---

# 14. Markets

Markets do not use their own decks.

Each settlement draws cards from the shared Loot Deck.

When a settlement is revealed, draw Loot Cards equal to that settlement's Market size and place them face up beside the settlement.

All displayed cards may be purchased by any character.

When a card is purchased:

1. Pay its currency cost.
2. Add the purchased card to the buyer's Inventory, if capacity allows.
3. Immediately draw 1 replacement card from the Loot Deck.

A market remains associated with its settlement after it is discovered.

---

## Specialty Discounts

A settlement specialty grants a discount to cards belonging to that specialty.

For example:

* Mage settlements discount Mage and Magic cards.
* Warrior settlements discount Warrior and martial cards.
* Rogue settlements discount Rogue and stealth cards.
* Cleric settlements discount Cleric and holy cards.
* Druid settlements discount Druid and nature cards.

A matching card costs 1 less currency.

A card's cost can never be reduced below 0.

Non-matching cards may still be purchased at full price.

For example, an Iron Sword or Leather Armor displayed in a Mage Town market may still be purchased. It simply receives no Mage discount.

> The exact tag list that determines whether a card belongs to Mage, Warrior, Rogue, Cleric, or Druid specialties is still pending.

---

## Specialty Training

A settlement specialty allows training with matching Skill Cards.

For example:

* Mage settlements provide Mage training.
* Warrior settlements provide Warrior training.
* Rogue settlements provide Rogue training.
* Cleric settlements provide Cleric training.
* Druid settlements provide Druid training.

A character wishing to equip an Off-Class Skill must visit a settlement with the matching specialty.

---

# 15. Healing and Medical Services

Healing rules are selected before the game begins.

## Easy Mode

At any settlement, a character may recover to full HP for free.

---

## Standard Mode

Settlements provide medical care for currency.

Medical care improves with settlement size:

| Settlement | Medical Service   |
| ---------- | ----------------- |
| Village    | Medicine Man      |
| Town       | Temple of Healing |
| City       | General Hospital  |

The exact healing cost, healing limits, and differences between these services are determined by the final Standard Mode healing table.

---

## Hardcore Mode

Settlements do not restore HP.

Characters may recover HP only through:

* Spells.
* Abilities.
* Potions.
* Other card effects.

Settlements may still provide any non-HP services allowed by the scenario.

---

# 16. Settlement Tile Setup

Before the game begins, the scenario determines how many Villages, Towns, and Cities are used.

Example:

* 4 Villages.
* 3 Towns.
* 1 City.

This creates 8 settlements total.

To select settlement tiles:

1. Randomly draw the required number of Village tiles from the 5 Village tiles.
2. Randomly draw the required number of Town tiles from the 5 Town tiles.
3. Randomly draw the required number of City tiles from the 5 City tiles.
4. Ensure every specialty appears at least once, unless the scenario says otherwise.
5. Shuffle the selected Settlement Tiles together.
6. Include them among the face-down map tiles during map setup.

Players do not know a settlement's location, size, or specialty until its tile is revealed.

---

# 17. Dungeons and Encounters

Entering a dungeon begins an Encounter.

Each dungeon has its own:

* Dungeon Deck.
* Dungeon Discard Pile.
* Enemy Front Row.
* Enemy Back Row.
* Dungeon objective.
* Encounter state.

A dungeon normally uses a themed Dungeon Deck, such as:

* Undead.
* Beasts.
* Fire.
* Bandits.
* Cultists.
* Demons.
* Machines.

A typical Dungeon Deck is expected to contain approximately 30 cards, but final deck size is determined by the dungeon type or scenario.

---

## Dungeon Loot Setup

At the beginning of the game or when a dungeon is prepared, shuffle a specified number of Loot Cards from the shared Loot Deck into that dungeon's Dungeon Deck.

The current recommended starting value is 5 Loot Cards per dungeon.

Dungeon loot may include:

* Equipment.
* Items.
* Currency.
* Skill Cards.
* Spell Cards.
* Ability Cards.
* Crafting materials.
* Transportation.
* Other rewards.

---

## Dungeon Turn Order

Each encounter round resolves in this order:

1. Draw and resolve 1 Dungeon Card.
2. Resolve all enemy turns.
3. Resolve all participating player character turns.
4. Resolve end-of-round effects, including Bleed Out.

---

## Dungeon Cards

Dungeon Cards may include:

* Enemies.
* Loot.
* Traps.
* Events.
* Objectives.
* Bosses.
* Scenario-specific effects.

Each Dungeon Card explains how it is resolved.

---

# 18. Encounter Layout

Each encounter uses 2 rows for the players and 2 rows for enemies.

```text
Player Front Row: [ ] [ ] [ ] [ ]
Player Back Row:  [ ] [ ] [ ] [ ]

Enemy Front Row:  [ ] [ ] [ ] [ ]
Enemy Back Row:   [ ] [ ] [ ] [ ]
```

Each row has 4 slots.

Each character, enemy, summon, pet, companion, or allied NPC occupies 1 slot.

A slot may contain only 1 card.

---

## Placing Characters

When a character enters a dungeon, place that Character Card in an empty Player Front Row or Player Back Row slot.

Characters may change rows during an encounter by spending an action.

---

## Placing Enemies

Enemy Cards list a preferred row.

When an Enemy Card is drawn:

1. Place it in an empty slot in its preferred row.
2. If that row is full, shuffle the Enemy Card back into the Dungeon Deck.

This rule makes row capacity a strategic part of combat.

---

# 19. Combat Targeting

## Front Row

A character or enemy in the Front Row may use:

* Melee attacks.
* Ranged attacks.
* Spells.
* Items.
* Abilities.

A Front Row card may be targeted by melee, ranged, spell, or special effects.

---

## Back Row

A character or enemy in the Back Row may use:

* Ranged attacks.
* Spells.
* Items.
* Abilities.

A Back Row card cannot normally use melee attacks unless a card says otherwise.

A Back Row card cannot normally be targeted by melee attacks.

---

## Melee Attacks

Melee attacks may target only cards in the opposing Front Row.

A melee attack cannot target the opposing Back Row unless a card specifically says otherwise.

---

## Ranged Attacks and Spells

Ranged attacks and spells may target cards in either opposing row unless a card says otherwise.

Examples include:

* Bows.
* Crossbows.
* Throwing weapons.
* Fireball.
* Lightning Bolt.
* Other ranged abilities.

---

# 20. Character Actions

Each participating character receives 1 action during the Player Character portion of an encounter round.

A character may use that action to do 1 of the following:

* Attack.
* Cast a spell.
* Use an equipped ability.
* Use a Tactical Reserve card.
* Move from the Front Row to the Back Row.
* Move from the Back Row to the Front Row.
* Retreat from the dungeon.
* Take another action permitted by a card.

Moving between rows uses the character's entire action unless an effect says otherwise.

Some cards may combine movement and another action.

Example:

> Charge Strike: Move from the Back Row to the Front Row, then deal 4 melee damage.

A card that grants an additional action or permits movement without spending an action overrides the normal 1-action rule.

---

# 21. Enemy Turns and Heat

Each Enemy Card lists one or more actions.

During an Enemy Turn, resolve that enemy's listed actions from top to bottom.

If an action's condition is not met or it has no legal target, skip that action and continue to the next listed action.

For example:

> Goblin Berserker
>
> 1. Berserker Rage: If this enemy has less than half its HP remaining, its melee attacks deal +1 damage this turn.
> 2. Club Smash: Deal 3 melee damage.

If the Goblin Berserker has less than half HP remaining, it first gains the bonus and then uses Club Smash for 4 damage.

---

## Heat

Heat represents how threatening a character appears to enemies.

Unless an Enemy Card says otherwise, an enemy targets the legal character with the highest Heat.

Characters gain Heat primarily by dealing damage.

The amount of Heat gained is defined by the Spell, Ability, Weapon, or other card effect that caused the damage.

When a character leaves a dungeon, remove all Heat tokens from that character.

> Rules for Heat ties, illegal targets, Downed targets, and Heat effects that do not come from damage are still pending.

---

# 22. Defeating Enemies

Enemies have HP printed on their cards.

Place Damage counters on an enemy when it takes damage.

When an enemy's Damage counters equal or exceed its HP:

1. That enemy is defeated.
2. Remove it from its row.
3. Place it in that dungeon's Dungeon Discard Pile.

Defeated enemies do not return during the current dungeon attempt unless a card says otherwise.

---

# 23. Retreating and Cooperative Dungeons

A character may retreat by spending an action unless a card says otherwise.

When a character retreats:

* Remove that character from the dungeon encounter.
* Remove all Heat from that character.
* Return that character to the world map outside the dungeon.

A dungeon remains active while at least 1 character remains inside it.

If all characters leave a dungeon, the dungeon resets according to that dungeon's reset rules.

---

## Joining an Active Dungeon

A character may enter a dungeon already being explored by another player.

When that character joins:

* Place the joining character in an empty Player Row slot.
* That character becomes a legal target for enemies.
* That character receives actions during future encounter rounds.
* The dungeon continues from its current state.

This allows players to assist allies, rescue Downed characters, or take over a difficult encounter.

---

## Multiple Active Dungeons

Multiple dungeons may be active at the same time.

Each active dungeon maintains its own:

* Dungeon Deck.
* Discard Pile.
* Enemy rows.
* Objective progress.
* Participating characters.

When multiple dungeons are active, resolve one complete encounter round in each active dungeon in a consistent player or table order.

---

# 24. Summons, Allies, and Companions

Summons, pets, companions, hirelings, and allied NPCs occupy normal Player Row slots.

They follow the rules on their cards.

If no eligible Player Row slot is available, a summon or ally cannot enter play unless its card says otherwise.

> Rules for summon actions, Heat, duration, ownership, and defeat are still pending.

# 25. Scenario and Difficulty Setup

Before starting a game, players choose or receive:

* Scenario or campaign.
* Map size.
* Number of dungeons.
* Number of Villages, Towns, and Cities.
* Dungeon types.
* Victory condition.
* Defeat condition.
* Healing mode.
* Death mode.
* Optional time limit.
* Optional dungeon spread rule.
* Other scenario-specific rules.

The scenario should also define the starting region, starting character equipment, starting cards, and any required setup components.

# 26. Accessibility Principle

Project S.T.A.C.K. is intended to be playable with accessible physical components and clear card text.

The game should favor:

* Direct instructions on cards.
* Predictable card layouts.
* Clear row and slot structure.
* Minimal hidden bookkeeping.
* Physical organization that can be identified by touch, labels, or accessible digital support.

Specific accessibility component standards are maintained separately from these core rules.
